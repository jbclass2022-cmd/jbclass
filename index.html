Experiment 1: Sound

A simple activity to help students connect vibration with the production of sound in a clear, visual way.

Aim

To understand how vibration produces sound.

Procedure

  1. Take a ruler and place one end on the edge of a table.
  2. Press the ruler gently and release it.
  3. Observe the vibration and listen carefully to the sound produced.

Experiment 2: Salt Saturation in Water

This experiment shows how a solute dissolves in a solvent up to a certain limit. When salt is added spoon by spoon and no more dissolves, the solution has reached saturation.

Aim

To determine the point at which common salt stops dissolving in water and to understand the formation of a saturated solution.

Materials Required

  1. Clean transparent glass or beaker
  2. About 100 mL water at room temperature
  3. Common salt
  4. Teaspoon or measuring spoon
  5. Stirring rod or spoon
  6. Notebook for observations

Procedure

  1. Pour about 100 mL of water into a clean glass or beaker.
  2. Add one spoon of salt and stir properly until the salt dissolves completely.
  3. Continue adding salt one spoon at a time, stirring after each addition.
  4. Carefully observe the solution after every addition.
  5. At a certain stage, some salt will remain undissolved even after continuous stirring.
  6. Record the spoon number or approximate amount of salt added when this first happens.
  7. Allow the mixture to stand for a short time and confirm that the extra salt settles at the bottom.

Observation / Important Parameter

The important parameter in this experiment is the amount of solute dissolved in a fixed quantity of water at a fixed temperature. Temperature should remain nearly constant because solubility changes with temperature.

Result

Salt dissolves in water only up to a certain limit. After that limit is reached, the extra salt remains undissolved at the bottom of the container.

Conclusion

The solution becomes saturated when it cannot dissolve any more salt at the given temperature. This shows that every solvent has a limited capacity to dissolve a solute under specific conditions.

What This Experiment Teaches

This activity teaches that dissolving is not unlimited. It helps students understand solution formation, the role of concentration, and the effect of fixed physical conditions such as temperature and quantity of solvent.

Scientific Term Learned

The main term obtained from this experiment is saturated solution. Related terms include solute, solvent, and solubility.

What It Explains About the Nature of Chemicals

This experiment explains that chemical substances interact in a measurable and limited way. Salt particles mix uniformly with water only until the solvent can no longer accommodate more dissolved particles. It demonstrates that the behavior of substances depends on their properties and on conditions such as temperature and concentration.

Experiment 3: Air Pressure

A classic classroom demonstration that shows how air, although invisible, can still apply force and create surprising results.

Aim

To understand the effect of air pressure.

Procedure

  1. Take a glass and fill it with water.
  2. Cover the mouth of the glass with a stiff piece of paper.
  3. Hold the paper firmly and invert the glass carefully.
  4. Observe how the paper stays in place due to air pressure.
